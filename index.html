Linear programming

Linear Programming is an optimization technique that aims to maximize or minimize a linear objective function subject to linear constraints

Objective Function: A linear function we want to optimize (e.g., minimize cost). Decision Variables: Values we can adjust (e.g., how many units of material to buy from each supplier). Constraints: Linear inequalities or equalities that limit our decisions (e.g., supply capacity, storage limits).

Demand Concrete demand: 500 units total over the project timeline. Steel demand: 300 units total over the project timeline. Costs Cost of Concrete from Supplier A: $50 per unit. Cost of Concrete from Supplier B: $55 per unit. Cost of Steel from Supplier A: $100 per unit. Cost of Steel from Supplier B: $95 per unit. Supplier Capacity Supplier A can provide up to 400 units of Concrete and 200 units of Steel. Supplier B can provide up to 300 units of Concrete and 300 units of Steel. Additional Constraints Storage Limit: The site can hold a maximum of 600 total units of material (concrete + steel) at any given time. Project Demand Must Be Met: Must meet or exceed the total demand for each material.

Textbooks & Foundational Works Hillier, F. S., & Lieberman, G. J. (2021). Introduction to Operations Research (11th Ed.). McGraw-Hill. A classic textbook covering the foundations of linear programming, duality, sensitivity analysis, and more. Winston, W. L. (2004). Operations Research: Applications and Algorithms (4th Ed.). Duxbury Press. Offers practical examples and in-depth explanations of LP formulations and solutions.

Arshinder, Kanda, & Deshmukh (2008). “Supply chain coordination: Perspectives, empirical studies and research directions.” International Journal of Production Economics, 115(2), 316–335. Discusses coordination mechanisms in supply chains, relevant for understanding how procurement optimization fits into a broader system. Lu, S., & Li, H. (2003). “Resource-activity critical path method for construction planning.” Construction Management and Economics, 21(2), 157–164. Shows how resource constraints tie into construction scheduling, linking LP-like methods to real-world project planning.
